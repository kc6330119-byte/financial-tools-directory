Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions about finding a financial advisor

A fiduciary financial advisor is legally obligated to act in your best interest at all times. Unlike brokers who only need to recommend "suitable" investments, fiduciaries must put your interests ahead of their own, including disclosing any conflicts of interest. Registered Investment Advisors (RIAs) and Certified Financial Planners (CFPs) when providing financial planning are held to the fiduciary standard. When searching for an advisor, asking "Are you a fiduciary?" is one of the most important questions you can ask.
Financial advisor fees vary by structure. Fee-only advisors typically charge 0.5%-1.5% of assets under management annually, flat fees of $1,000-$5,000 per financial plan, or hourly rates of $150-$400. Fee-based advisors may also earn commissions on certain products. Commission-only advisors earn money from product sales. Always ask about total costs including fund expense ratios and any transaction fees. Many advisors offer a free initial consultation.
The most respected credentials include CFP (Certified Financial Planner) for comprehensive financial planning, CFA (Chartered Financial Analyst) for investment management expertise, and CPA (Certified Public Accountant) for tax-focused planning. Other valuable designations include ChFC, RICP for retirement income, and CLU for insurance planning. Always verify that an advisor is registered with the SEC or their state securities regulator and check their disciplinary history on FINRA BrokerCheck.
Consider hiring a financial advisor during major life transitions: getting married, having children, receiving an inheritance, changing jobs (especially with equity compensation), approaching retirement, or managing a windfall. You may also benefit from an advisor if you have complex tax situations, multiple accounts to coordinate, or simply want professional guidance to stay on track with your financial goals. Even a one-time financial plan review can provide valuable direction.
